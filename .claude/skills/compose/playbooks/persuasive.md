# Persuasive Long-Form — Format Playbook

For external-facing persuasive content: landing pages, sales pages, nurture sequences, marketing collateral. Content that needs to move a reader from one belief to another.

**Voice stack at this point:** Voice base (loaded) → client kernel (loaded) → THIS playbook.

---

## Core Distinction

This is NOT a data writeup or a report. Persuasive content has a TRANSFORMATION goal — the reader should believe something different after reading. The structure exists to move them through an emotional arc, not to organize information.

---

## Structure Follows Argument

The Key Moves from the Copy Intent (Step 2) become the structural spine. Each section exists to make one persuasive move. If a section doesn't advance the argument, it doesn't exist.

**Do NOT impose a template.** Sales page formulas (PAS, AIDA, StoryBrand) are reference tools for understanding persuasion mechanics, not fill-in-the-blank frameworks. The operator's convictions and the reader's psychology determine the arc. Discover the structure from the moves.

---

## Opening Formula

The opening earns the next sentence. Nothing else.

**Patterns that work:**
- **Provocative observation:** "Less experienced marketers with better systems are outproducing more talented people without them." — states a reality the reader recognizes.
- **Concrete demonstration:** "I prepped for a client meeting in five minutes. Not a demo — a real meeting, real client, real stakes." — first-person walkthrough.
- **Consequence-first:** "Your week 10 newsletter is better than week 1 — because the system learned what resonates." — states the outcome.

**What kills openings:**
- Background context ("In today's marketing landscape...")
- Defining the problem generically ("Many marketers struggle with...")
- Feature announcements ("Introducing our new...")
- Questions ("Are you tired of...?" "What if I told you...?")

---

## Section Architecture

Each section makes one persuasive move from the Copy Intent. Within each section:

1. **Hook** — the first line earns attention. Observation, consequence, or provocation. Not a topic sentence.
2. **Demonstration** — show the thing working. Specific scenario, real numbers, walkthrough. This is the proof.
3. **Implication** — what this means for the reader. State it, don't argue it. Let the demonstration carry the weight.

**Transitions between sections:** The end of one section creates the question that the next section answers. Don't use transition phrases ("Moving on to...", "Another benefit is..."). Let the argument's logic create the flow.

---

## Length Budget

**Landing page:** 1,500-3,000 words. Enough to make the full argument; short enough to hold attention.

**Sales page (long-form):** 3,000-5,000 words. Full argument with multiple proof points and objection handling.

**Nurture email:** 200-500 words per email. Each email makes ONE move. The sequence makes the full argument across multiple emails.

**The test:** Can you state what each section DOES (not what it says) in one sentence? If not, the section is doing too much.

---

## Proof Standards

Every persuasive move needs credibility. Pull from loaded source material:

| Proof Type | When to Use | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Specific result | Always — the strongest proof | "Meeting debrief: 3 minutes, 6 outputs. One transcript drop." |
| Named tool/method | When the specificity adds credibility | "Claude Code, HubSpot, Instantly, Clay" |
| Time/money comparison | When the reader feels the gap | "What used to require a $60-80K/year hire" |
| Operator experience | When authenticity matters | "300+ sessions. 3 clients." |
| Before/after | When the transformation is vivid | "Your first newsletter: 2 hours. Week 10: 10 minutes." |

**If proof doesn't exist in the loaded docs:** Flag it. "We need proof for [claim] — what do you have?" Don't fabricate. The audience can smell it.

---

## Tone Calibration (Persuasive-Specific)

Beyond the voice base and kernel:

- **Conviction, not hype.** The operator believes what they're saying because they've done the work. Let the specifics carry the energy. Don't manufacture urgency.
- **Showing IS the argument.** A walkthrough of the meeting debrief workflow is more persuasive than "our system saves you hours." Show the thing working, and the argument makes itself.
- **Acknowledge the skeptic.** The audience has heard every AI promise. Meet that skepticism head-on.
- **End earned, not imposed.** The CTA lands because the argument earned it. If the reader isn't ready to act after the content, the argument failed — the CTA can't fix it.

---

## Composition Method (Two-Pass)

**Pass 1 — Structure lock:**
1. Write the opening hook (earns the next sentence).
2. Map Key Moves to sections — one move per section.
3. Set length target.
4. Verify: does the emotional arc progress? Does each section create the question the next answers?

**Pass 2 — Draft in batches:**
Work 2-3 sections at a time. Present to operator. Iterate before continuing.

For each section:
- Does the hook earn attention?
- Is the demonstration specific and real?
- Does the implication land without being stated too explicitly?
- Am I composing from the operator's language or generating generic copy?

---

## Anti-Patterns

- **Feature lists disguised as persuasion.** "Feature 1... Feature 2..." is a spec sheet. Each section should make a MOVE, not describe a capability.
- **Problem-agitate-solve as a rigid formula.** PAS works as a lens, not a template. If the structure feels like a formula, it is one.
- **Benefit inflation.** "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "transformative" — the banned words exist because persuasive copy is where they're most tempting. Resist.
- **Generic social proof.** "Trusted by thousands" means nothing. Specific numbers from real work are what count.
- **CTA before the argument lands.** If you're placing CTAs every 3 paragraphs, the argument isn't strong enough to hold attention. Fix the argument.
