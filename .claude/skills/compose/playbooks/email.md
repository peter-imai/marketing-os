# Email Format Playbook

For client emails: meeting recaps, follow-ups, introductions, status updates, operational messages. Loaded by `/compose` when the email format is selected.

**Voice stack at this point:** Voice base (loaded) → client kernel (loaded) → THIS playbook.

---

## Core Principle

**Emails are working documents, not performances.** Every email either advances understanding, moves a decision forward, or creates clarity about what happens next. No email should exist because it "feels like we should send something."

---

## Opening Formula

The opening is the most consequential choice. The first substantive sentence (after greeting) IS the content.

**Pattern:** Lead with the most important thing the reader needs to know.

| Email Type | Opening Pattern | Example |
|-----------|----------------|---------|
| Meeting recap | Gratitude + substance | "Thanks for the time today — a substantive discussion." |
| Status update | One-line framing | "The 1K pilot is done — results met all three targets." |
| Follow-up | Context refresh + ask | "Following up on the TAM analysis from Tuesday." |
| Operational | Direct to substance | "In order to build Rhonda's dream — infrastructure capable of sending 10k emails/week, the math breaks down like this:" |

**Never:** "I hope this email finds you well." "Per our conversation." "As discussed." "Just circling back."

## Structure by Email Type

### Meeting Recap

1. **Greeting** — casual, one sentence.
2. **What we covered** — bold section headers, one paragraph per topic. Each paragraph leads with the key finding or decision, then supporting detail.
3. **Next steps** — separated by whose court the ball is in ("On our side:" vs. "For the team:")
4. **Questions** — numbered, each scoped to a specific knowledge holder.
5. **Close** — one sentence pointing forward, then sign-off.

**Length:** 300-500 words. Enough to be the record; short enough for a phone.

**Subject:** "Call wrap - [date]" or "[Context] – Meeting Recap & Action Items ([date])"

### Follow-Up

1. **Greeting** — context refresh ("Following up on [topic] from [when].")
2. **The ask** — state it clearly. Don't bury it.
3. **Context** — why it matters or what depends on it.
4. **Timeline** — when you need it by, and why that date.
5. **Close.**

**Length:** 100-200 words. Shorter is better.

### Status Update

1. **Greeting** — why you're writing.
2. **Headline** — the most important thing in one sentence.
3. **Detail** — supporting context, bold headers if multi-topic.
4. **What's next.**
5. **Close.**

**Length:** 200-350 words. Scale with the substance.

### Introduction / Outreach

1. **How you know them** or why you're reaching out — one sentence.
2. **What you're proposing** — direct.
3. **Why it's relevant to THEM** — demonstrate homework.
4. **One clear next step.**
5. **Close.**

**Length:** 100-150 words. Long introductions don't get read.

### Operational / Action Item

Jump straight to substance. No preamble. Numbers, costs, steps. Clear next action.

**Length:** Scale to content. Math shown, not just answers.

### Reply / Thread Continuation

- Match the thread's register.
- Address their points before adding new info.
- If thread is long, summarize state first.
- Don't re-introduce context the thread already has.
- Match their length.

## Formatting Rules

- **Bold for section headers:** "**What we covered:**" "**Next steps:**"
- **Italic for item labels:** "*CRM audit and cleanup.* We'll pull your HubSpot data..."
- **Numbered lists for questions** — easy to reference in replies ("re: question 3...")
- **Bullets for next steps** — scannable, parallel structure
- **Inline document links** with descriptive labels, not raw URLs
- **Exclamation points fine in casual contexts** — quick replies, genuine energy. Not for hype.
- **No emojis.** Professional register.
- **Plain text preferred.** Not HTML newsletters.
- **One blank line between sections.**

## Closing Patterns

| Pattern | Example | When |
|---------|---------|------|
| Casual forward | "Talk soon," / "Chat soon!" | Default / recurring |
| Action-oriented | "Let me know if any questions — otherwise we'll proceed." | Clear next steps |
| Open invitation | "Happy to jump on a call if easier." | Complex topics |
| Routing | "If anything comes up before Monday, Slack is fastest." | Practical |
| Team sign-off | "— Alex, Sarah, Mike" | Full team |
| ps. afterthought | "ps. I'll start posting agendas to the calendar invite..." | Practical add-on |

**Sign-off:** First name only. Signature block in Gmail signature, not body. Exception: first email to new contact — include "Name / Company / phone" inline.

## Specificity Standard

| Instead of... | Use... |
|--------------|--------|
| "We've made good progress" | "We sized the TAM at 80,000-120,000 companies across eight verticals" |
| "Several companies match" | "~20,000 manufacturing companies with active DoD contracts" |
| "The timeline works well" | "November 2026 CMMC deadline gives us 8 months of lead time" |
| "We'll look into it" | "I'll have the vertical scoring framework to you by Thursday" |

**Name people in the body.** "As Julianne noted..." "In order to build Rhonda's dream..."

**"My ask:" framing.** When the email contains both information and a request, separate them clearly.

**Acknowledge before asking.** Name constraints before making requests: "I know we're waiting on clearance for Hubspot, but..."

## Composition Method (Two-Pass)

**Pass 1 — Structure lock:**
1. Write the opening line (consequence-first after greeting).
2. List section headers for this email type.
3. Set length target for this type.

**Pass 2 — Draft:**
Fill in each section. Present as one complete email with metadata header.

---

## Pre-Send Checklist

- [ ] Does every paragraph earn its place?
- [ ] Are strong claims specific?
- [ ] Is the ask clear?
- [ ] Right recipients (To vs. CC)?
- [ ] Subject line tells the reader what the email IS?
- [ ] First sentence sounds like the operator, not a template?
- [ ] Shortest version that has everything it needs?
- [ ] Close matches the register?
