# Marketing Patterns Catalog

Named, attributed, opinionated, cross-channel marketing methods. Each pattern is a proven method that works across multiple channels, has a real attributed author, articulates explicit opinions, and resolves visible tensions (Forces).

This is one of the most powerful capabilities in the system. Over time, as you capture methods from practitioners you learn from, this catalog becomes a compounding asset -- a library of battle-tested approaches you can deploy and adapt to any engagement.

---

## What Belongs Here

**Marketing patterns** are named, attributed, cross-cutting methods with these properties:

- **Named and attributed.** Someone invented this. It has a lineage, not just "best practices."
- **Cross-channel.** The method's description makes sense without naming a specific channel. If you can't describe it without saying "in cold email," it's discipline-specific and belongs in the practice area doc, not here.
- **Opinionated.** The method makes specific epistemic commitments -- definitional moves that separate it from generic advice.
- **Forces are visible.** The method resolves competing tensions (forces). Forces are why the method works the way it does, not just what it does.

**Discipline-specific tactics** (single-channel, platform-specific) stay in their practice area doc in `resources/marketing/`. The distinction is scope: a pattern card should make sense across channels; a tactic is bound to one.

## Pattern Card Structure

Every pattern gets its own file with 13 required sections. This structure makes patterns comparable, composable, and learnable.

| Section | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Attribution** | Who created this. Lineage and source. |
| **Intent** | What the method is for -- one sentence. |
| **Context** | When to apply (and when NOT to). Conditions, prerequisites, contraindications. |
| **Problem** | The fuzzy question this pattern makes answerable. |
| **Forces** | The competing tensions the method resolves. Each force pulls in a direction; the procedure is a resolution. This is the hardest section and the most valuable. |
| **Core Opinions** | The epistemic commitments -- specific definitional moves that separate this from generic advice. |
| **Procedure** | The mechanical steps. Specific numbers, specific decisions. |
| **What It Produces** | Concrete outputs you walk away with. |
| **Known Variants & Limits** | Adaptations and where the pattern breaks. |
| **What It Is NOT** | Negative space -- sharpens identity against adjacent methods. |
| **Related Patterns** | Ancestors, cousins, children, siblings. How this connects to other methods. |
| **Known Uses / Stamps** | Bidirectional links to real executions. Each stamp records the engagement, calibration choices, and link to the artifact. This is the compounding mechanism -- patterns get smarter through use. |
| **Provenance** | Where the captured material came from. Source integrity. |

**On Forces:** Forces are the hardest part to get right and the most valuable part of the pattern. A force is a tension between competing values that the method's solution resolves. "Enough signal to trust the read vs. as few emails as possible to get it" is a force. "Be data-driven" is not -- it's a platitude. Every force should trace to something specific in the source material. Forces drafted from general knowledge rather than real practitioner experience are the failure mode.

## How to Use This Catalog

1. **Browse** to find methods relevant to your current problem class.
2. **Read the Forces first** -- they tell you whether the method addresses the tensions you're actually facing.
3. **Execute the Procedure** with your engagement's specifics.
4. **Stamp the execution** back to the pattern. One-line entry in Known Uses: session, client, calibration notes, link to your execution artifact. The pattern gets smarter; your future self gets a reference.

## Building Your Pattern Library

Patterns accumulate through practice. Here is how they typically enter the system:

1. **You learn a method from a practitioner** -- conference talk, course, advisor session, podcast. The method has a name, an author, and specific opinions about how things should be done.
2. **You notice it's cross-channel.** The structure works regardless of whether you're doing email, LinkedIn, paid, or direct mail. The channel is a calibration variable, not the definition.
3. **You capture it.** Create a new file in this directory following the pattern card structure. Forces first -- if you can't articulate the tensions the method resolves, it's not ready to be a pattern yet.
4. **You execute it.** Apply the pattern to a real engagement. Stamp the execution.
5. **It compounds.** Next time you face a similar problem, you have a proven method with real execution data. The pattern catalog is a growing competitive advantage.

The specimen pattern (`situation-variant-testing.md`) shows what a complete pattern looks like. Use it as your template.
